Fundamental Structural
Systems in Analysis

Beams, frames, and trusses form the foundation of structural analysis in
civil engineering. Understanding how each system responds to loads is
essential for applying analytical methods such as the slope-deflection
method and interpreting internal force diagrams correctly.

Beams: Load-Bearing Members Resisting Bending

A beam resists transverse loads causing shear and bending. Common in floors, bridges, and roofs, beams can be simply supported, fixed, or cantilevered.

Frames: Beams and Columns Resisting Multi-Directional Loads

A frame is a network of beams and columns that resists bending, shear, and axial forces. Used in buildings and high-rises, analysis often requires methods like slope-deflection.

Trusses: Triangular Assemblies Efficiently Carrying Axial Forces

A frame is a network of beams and columns that resists bending, shear, and axial forces. Used in buildings and high-rises, analysis often requires methods like slope-deflection.

Introduction and Concept

Slope-deflection analysis is a fundamental method used to study statistically indeterminate structures, such as continuous beams and rigid frames. It helps engineers determine how structures deform under various loads by relating end moments to the rotations and displacements of members. Unlike simple beams, where internal forces can be determined directly from equilibrium, frames and multi-span beams require consideration of joint rotations and member interactions, which makes slope-deflection essential for accurate analysis.

Slope-Deflection Equations

Slope-deflection equations are the core of slope-deflection analysis. They express the moments at the ends of a member in terms of end rotations, relative displacements, and fixed-end moments caused by external loads. This approach is especially useful for continuous beams and rigid frames, where multiple spans and interconnected members make direct equilibrium analysis insufficient. Consider a beam member AB of length L with flexural rigidity EI. If the ends of the member rotate by angles θA and θB​, the moments at the ends are given by the slope-deflection equations:

MAB​=MFAB + EI/​L(2θA​+θB​-3△/L)

MBA​=MFBA + EI/​L(θA​+2θB​-3△/L)

Slope-deflection analysis is widely used for:

  • Continuous beams over multiple supports
  • Rigid frames in buildings and bridges
  • Structures with combined loading and displacements

Advantages

  • Accurate for indeterminate structures.
  • Can incorporate support settlements and releases.
  • Provides direct member end moments, useful for design.

Limitations

  • Assumes linear elastic behavior.
  • Calculation can be tedious for large structures, though programmable.

Project Overview

It is a semester project carried out by a group of eleven 400-level Civil and Environmental Engineering students at the University of Lagos. The aim is to develop a web application for CEG 410 that helps students and engineers analyze beams, frames, and trusses using the Slope-Deflection Method. The application enables users to input structural data and compute internal moments, shear forces, and deflections, with results visualized through Bending Moment Diagrams, Shear Force Diagrams, and deflected shapes. In addition, the site provides educational resources, including embedded video tutorials, to explain the slope-deflection method and support learning. This project bridges theory and practical application, creating a hands-on platform that enhances understanding of structural analysis while supporting real-world engineering practice.
